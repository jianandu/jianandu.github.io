My research sits at the intersection of comparative politics, Chinese politics, and political methodology. I study how historical cultural legacies—particularly premodern beliefs about fate, fortune, and the supernatural—continue to influence bureaucratic decision-making in contemporary authoritarian states.

---

## Working Papers

### Superstition and Bureaucratic Competition (with Junyan Jiang)

Why does superstition persist within a highly atheist government? This project examines how bureaucratic competition under authoritarian rule creates fertile ground for supernatural beliefs. Using original data on Chinese officials' geomantic practices, I show that officials facing intense career competition are more likely to adopt superstitious behaviors as a response to uncertainty.

## Research Agenda

I am developing a broader research agenda on the **cultural foundations of authoritarian governance**. My work asks:

1. **How do premodern beliefs persist?** Tracing the transmission of religious and supernatural ideas from imperial China to the modern bureaucratic apparatus.

2. **How do beliefs affect policy?** Examining how officials' private beliefs—about Feng Shui, lucky dates, fortune-telling—influence concrete policy outcomes in areas like urban planning, public infrastructure, and personnel decisions.

3. **What are the institutional consequences?** Understanding how the interaction between formal institutions and informal beliefs shapes governance quality, corruption patterns, and policy implementation.

## Methods

I employ a range of quantitative and computational approaches:

- **Text analysis**: Mining official documents, speeches, and historical archives
- **Statistical modeling**: Causal inference with observational data
- **Computational methods**: Machine learning for pattern detection in large datasets
